From Independence to Correlation: A 2D Gaussian Visualization

Instructions

This experiment visualizes the effect of correlation on the joint probability distribution of a noise signal.

The 2D Joint Distribution

  • The main plot shows the 2D joint probability distribution of the noise signal at the current time, \(X(t)\), versus the signal at a delayed time, \(X(t-\tau)\).
  • Uncorrelated Noise (White): When any two samples are statistically independent, their joint distribution is circularly symmetric. The probability of a certain value for \(X(t)\) does not depend on the value of \(X(t-\tau)\).
  • Correlated Noise (Colored): When samples are correlated, the distribution becomes a tilted ellipse. This shows that the value of \(X(t)\) is statistically dependent on \(X(t-\tau)\). For example, a high value at \(t-\tau\) makes a high value at \(t\) more likely.

Generalization to N-Dimensions

While we can only visualize two variables at a time, this concept extends to any number of dimensions. The relationship between \(n\) random variables is fully described by an \(n \times n\) covariance matrix. The circular (independent) and elliptical (correlated) contours you see here are simply the 2D representations of that matrix.

How to Interact

  • Use the "Correlation Strength" slider to apply a filter that introduces correlation between time samples.
  • Observe how the contours change from perfect circles (\(\rho = 0\)) for uncorrelated noise to tilted ellipses (\(\rho > 0\)) as you increase the correlation.
  • The Status Box provides the theoretical correlation coefficient, \(\rho\), for the current filter setting.
